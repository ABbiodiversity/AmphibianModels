Using Autonomous Recording Units to develop species distribution models for Amphibians in Alberta, Canada

4 Species Distribution Models

4.1 Landcover information

Landcover information was extracted from the ABMI detailed Wall-to-Wall vegetation and Human Footprint GIS layer. The proportional area of vegetation with broad stand age classes and human disturbance classes were determined within a 150m buffer by manual interpretation of 1:30,000 aerial photos and SPOT satellite imagery. In regions where vegetation classes do not capture the variation in species distributions, the proportional area of soils, classified into seven broad categories, based on a simplified version of the Agricultural Region of Alberta Soil Inventory Database was used. In addition, we included a term to capture if a site was naturally treed (probability of aspen). Climatic variables were derived from historical weather station data (500 m2 spatial resolution) using the parameter-elevation regressions on independent slopes model (PRISM) method. As amphibians require water for successful breeding, we also calculated the proportion of water within a 1 km2 buffer around each site.To enable provincial wide predictions, the above information was also summarizes as a 1 km2 raster for the entire province.

SDMs were created for all species which were detected in at least 20 ARUs within the analysis region (vegetation or soil) using a model selection framework. We created a set of statistcal models (binomial generalized linear models) to relate the relative abundance (presence/absence) of each species to two sets of variables: landcover (vegetation, soil or human footprint) and climate (climate normals and spatial location). This framework allowed us to create models for a large number of species while selecting models for each species that are only as complex as the data supports. For example, the models for a rarer species with few records may not be able to separate different age classes or even broad stand types. In these cases, all age classes or several stand types therefore have the same average value in the resulting habitat model.

For those looking for more technical details, the src file in this repository contains all scripts used to develop these models. Raw data can be acquired by contacting Brandon Allen (brandon.allen@ualberta.ca)

4.2 Reporting Regions

We summarize our SDM for two reporting regions: 1) The Forested region is composed of the Boreal, Canadian Shield, Rocky Mountain (excluding eastern portion of the montane region), and Foothills natural regions in addition to the northern portion of the Dry Mixedwood natural subregion. These areas are predominately impacted by forestry footprint; 2) The Prairie region is composed of the Grassland and Parkland natural regions, the southern portion of the Dry Mixedwood natural subregion, and the eastern portion of the Montane natural subregion. These areas were grouped together due to agriculture being the predominate anthropogenic disturbance.

The boundaries of the “forested” and “prairie” reporting regions used for calculating sector effects.
